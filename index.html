Amid criticism, how does New York City's Renewal Schools Program measure up?

By: Chris Hickey

The New York City Department of Education deems 78 of its middle and high schools as "Renewal" schools. This means they've been the beneficiaries of 582 million dollars in extra funding to support social services and academics. It's an effort to improve learning because their students have consistently scored poorly on state Math and English Language Arts (ELA) assessment tests.

Mayor Bill De Blasio's administration, under the stewardship of former schools Chancellor Carmen Fariña, launched the Renewal Schools Program at 94 campuses in late 2014. The city established a policy that if the schools did not improve substantially within three years, they would be closed.

Since 2014, the program has achieved mixed results. While most of the students at the renewal schools improved their test scores in reading and math last year, nearly all the schools still lag behind citywide averages in test scores. Nearly half of the high schools in the program did not meet 4-year or 6-year graduation targets.

Twenty-one of the schools will be moved out of the program next year because they've improved. The Department of Education dubs these "Rise" schools. But DOE also announced recently that it would shutter five of the schools by next year and merge five more because they've failed to measure up to expectations. Some critics have since argued the program hasn't been very successful.

A June 2017 study authored by Aaron Pallas of Columbia University's Teachers College compared test scores and graduation rate data from 78 of the schools in the program to 90 other struggling schools with similar demographics that weren't in the program. Pallas told Chalkbeat that "based on the evidence I see… the program is not having a meaningful impact on academic outcomes."

Another June 2017 study by the conservative Manhattan Institute came to slightly different conclusions. Researcher Marcus Winters found that schools in the Renewal Program had indeed generally improved outcomes, but at a significant cost.

"The magnitude of RSP's positive average effect is meaningful. However, the effect is statistically insignificant for certain grades in math and ELA; and it is statistically insignificant for ELA when renewal schools are compared only with nonrenewal schools that were low-performing as of 2013," the study says.

Winters questioned whether the 1.4 million additional dollars invested per school was worth it.

"In short, these findings suggest that New York's Renewal School Program has boosted student achievement, on average, in some of the city's worst schools; but RSP has done so at exceptionally high cost, especially when compared with the low-cost, highly effective, alternative of swiftly closing ineffective schools."

New schools Chancellor Richard Carranza, who was hired in March, has expressed his support for continuing the Renewal Program, but it's still unclear how long it will last.
